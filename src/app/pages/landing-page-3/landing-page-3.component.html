Why Playing The Ball Off Your Left Foot Isn't Enough To Hit Longer Drives

By: Dustin D. May 3, 2026 In Partnership With Gentle Gator Golf

Ball position has to match up with stance width and spine tilt to get the benefit

I used to think that as long as I played the ball off my left heel, I was doing everything I needed to do to hit up on the ball. We've all heard it: "Put the ball forward to hit up on it."

But no matter how far forward I moved the ball up, my drives were still ballooning and falling short with almost zero roll out. No matter how much I tried to "feel" like I was swinging up, I just couldn't get the ball to launch higher with the knuckle spin we all chase. Confused, I went back to the drawing board and started analyzing the swings of the longest hitters on tour. What I found was shocking: Playing the ball forward isn't enough.

Hitting Up = Less Spin = More Distance

Hitting down on the ball results in a lower launch angle with high spin costing golfers serious yardage.

1. You're Likely Too Narrow

Most golfers stand with their feet too close together. When your stance is narrow, you end up "stacked" on top of the ball like a short iron. This forces a steep descent, resulting in weak, spinning slices.

2. The "Right Foot Drop Fix"

To fix your launch angle, drop your right foot back until the inside of your foot is shoulder-width apart. This creates the platform needed to swing up on the ball. Don't overdo it, or you'll bottom out early and "top" the ball.

A positive angle of attack is required to maximize your driving distance

3. The Visual Illusion No One Tells You

Now that you have a perfect stance width to hit up on the ball you have to overcome the visual illusion that tricks golfers into hitting slices when set up correctly. Your head will now be much further back than you are used to. Making aiming the driver really difficult. To add to the problem driver faces are curved with no flat edges. Squaring one up by eye is literally a guessing game. You might feel aligned, but your eyes are lying to you.

Drivers have no flat edges making it impossible to aim without a lot of trial and error

4. The "Six Degree" Disaster

One tiny "tick" on a clock face is 6 degrees. At driver speeds, that tiny error sends your ball over 40 yards off target. If you can't see exactly where you are aimed, you've lost the hole before you swing.

Face pointers make seeing where your clubface is pointing fast and easy

5. Why Magnetic Face Pointers Fail

Traditional face pointers have been around for years, but they have a major flaw: they rely on magnets. Today's high-performance drivers are made of titanium and carbon fiber, which aren't magnetic. This makes old-school training aids useless for the modern golfer.
